Why we built WayPoint

We built WayPoint because independence matters. So many navigation apps were made for people who can see — full of tiny menus, complex maps, and walls of text that are impossible to use with a screen reader or when your hands are full holding a cane.

We imagined a grandmother who memorized every step from her front door to her favorite bakery. We imagined a teenager learning a new college campus. We imagined a father who wanted to explore his neighborhood without asking for help every five minutes.

WayPoint is for them. It lets anyone set personal notes at real-world locations — things like "sharp corner here, slow down" or "this is my favorite seat at the coffee shop." When you walk near that spot, your phone speaks the note to you. No maps to read. No tiny buttons to tap. Just your own voice, waiting for you.

We believe everyone deserves to move through the world with confidence. WayPoint is our way of helping make that happen.

How to use WayPoint
1
Open the app
Tap the big "Open My WayPoints" button at the top of this page to get started.
2
Add a waypoint
Tap the pink "+ Add" button and give the spot a name and a note — say anything that will help you later.
3
Set your range
Choose how close you need to be before your phone announces the note — from 5 to 100 meters away.
4
Add sub-notes
Inside a waypoint, add smaller notes like "favorite corner seat" or "step at entrance."
5
Hear it speak
Walk near a saved spot and your phone will read your note aloud automatically — no tapping needed.
6
Tap Read anytime
Select any waypoint and press the blue "Read" button to hear its note immediately, on demand.
🎤
Voice commands

A note for family & helpers

If you are setting up WayPoint for someone you love, walk through their most important routes with them. Stop at every place that matters — the bakery, the bus stop, the tricky curb outside the pharmacy — and add a waypoint together.

Let them record the note in their own words. Their own descriptions will mean the most to them. You can always come back later to add more detail.

The best waypoints are specific. Instead of "coffee shop," try "Green Door Café — three steps up at the entrance, order at the counter straight ahead, my usual is an oat latte." The more detail, the more confident and independent they will feel.

You can also add hazard waypoints for dangerous spots — sharp corners, broken pavements, busy driveways — so the app warns them before they get there. These small notes can make a huge difference.
